I Checked 15 Organizations' Traffic. Only One Had Meaningful Paid Search.

Widi Ginanjar · SEO & Growth · July 25, 2026 · 6 min read

I got curious one evening and started checking traffic breakdowns for a handful of organizations, starting with the one I used to work for. Then I kept going. Fifteen organizations later, ranging from a 400-visit local foundation to Airbnb at 80 million, one pattern held almost everywhere: direct and organic search dominate, paid search barely registers.

The bottom line: fourteen out of fifteen organizations get found the same way, through search or through people who already know the name. One organization, Delterra, breaks that pattern, and the reason isn't age, size, or sector. It's a single, specific choice.

A note on the sample before the data. Most of what follows is nonprofits, since that's where the question started. But a pattern that only shows up in nonprofits could mean the pattern is about nonprofits specifically, or it could mean nonprofits just happen to share some other trait, budget constraints, for instance, that produces the same result for an unrelated reason. To tell those two explanations apart, I deliberately added two organizations that are not nonprofits at all: HubSpot, a company with a large, well-documented marketing budget, and Airbnb, one of the highest-traffic consumer sites in the world. If the pattern held for them too, that would be evidence the pattern isn't really about nonprofit status. It did.

One more limit worth stating plainly: every number here is a single snapshot, checked on one day in July 2026, not a trend measured over months. Delterra's 30.3% paid traffic could be an active, short-term campaign rather than a permanent feature of how it operates. If I checked again in three months, some of these numbers would likely move. The pattern across fourteen organizations is wide enough that I'd expect it to hold. The Delterra number specifically, being the one exception, is the one I'd trust least as a stable, ongoing fact rather than a moment I happened to catch.

The one exception

Fourteen organisations sit under 2% paid traffic, most well under 1%. Even HubSpot, a company known for aggressive marketing spend, shows just 0.58% paid on 49.7 million visits.

Then there's Delterra, at 30.3%. Every other organisation on this list is a rounding error by comparison.

My first theory didn't hold up

My first guess was age. Delterra spun out as an independent nonprofit in 2021, decades younger than Red Cross or WWF, so maybe younger organisations lean on paid search while older ones coast on accumulated backlinks and brand recognition.

Then I checked charity: water, founded in 2006, roughly a quarter the age of Red Cross. Its paid traffic was 0.55%, in line with the century-old organisations, not the newer one. Age alone doesn't explain it.

What actually might be going on

Here's what makes the near-universal low paid traffic more surprising, not less. Google gives eligible nonprofits up to $10,000 a month in free search advertising through its Ad Grants program, no cash spend required, just eligibility and compliance. Money isn't the barrier it would be for a for-profit company deciding whether to buy ads.

And yet even organisations that could plausibly be using that free grant still show almost no paid traffic in the data. I don't know why. Maybe some aren't enrolled. Maybe the traffic from Ad Grants gets classified differently by the tool I was using. Maybe running Ad Grants well takes more ongoing management than most nonprofit teams have time for, since Google requires accounts to maintain a 5% click-through rate to stay compliant.

Delterra looks less like a pattern and more like a single organisation's specific decision, one campaign or one push that the other fourteen didn't happen to be running the week I checked.

A second number that points the same way

Paid traffic wasn't the only place Delterra stood out. Its direct traffic, people who type the site's address straight into their browser instead of searching for it, was 5.09%. Every other organisation on the list was higher, most well above 50%.

Low direct traffic usually means one simple thing: people don't know the name yet. They're not typing "delterra.org" the way someone might type "unicef.org". They find the site through a search or through an ad instead.

Put the two numbers together and the story gets clearer. Low direct traffic plus high paid traffic looks like an organisation still building the kind of name recognition that Red Cross or UNICEF built up over decades. That's not a problem. It's just an earlier stage.

The honest conclusion

I didn't find a clean rule here. What I found is that almost every organisation I checked, regardless of age, size, or sector, gets found the same way: people either already know the name, or they find it through unpaid search. Given that most nonprofits still don't have anyone dedicated to thinking about their own SEO, that's either reassuring or slightly alarming, depending on how much of that organic visibility they can actually explain.
